\begin{abstract}
    Computer vision analysis in aquatic environments presents unique challenges due to refraction, turbulence, and occlusion, which hinder the accuracy of standard motion tracking systems. 
    This project proposes a non-intrusive AI-based system for stroke phase detection and symmetry analysis in competitive swimming. 
    The core contribution is a novel methodology for dynamic pixel-to-meter scaling using standard lane ropes as calibration objects, addressing the limitations of fixed-camera setups in varying pool environments. 
    This progress report outlines the comparative analysis between traditional computer vision pipelines and Convolutional Neural Networks for feature extraction. 
    It presents the system architecture, preliminary findings on lane rope calibration, and the evaluation plan utilizing both quantitative error metrics and qualitative expert feedback.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
Performance analysis in competitive swimming is traditionally reliant on manual observation or expensive, sensor-based systems that can be intrusive to the athlete. 
While computer vision offers a non-intrusive alternative, the aquatic environment introduces significant noise. 
Light refraction at the air-water interface, surface turbulence caused by the swimmer, and occlusions (bubbles and splashes) degrade the quality of visual data~\cite{ahmed2019, giulietti2023}. 
Furthermore, camera distances and angles vary significantly between venues, making consistent metric quantification (e.g., speed in meters/second) difficult without distinct calibration markers.

\subsection{Motivation}
There is a distinct lack of standardized, automated systems for extracting swimming metrics from video footage that do not require complex setups. 
A standard, simple, and well documented system capable of extracting metric data from video with minimal setup could act as a proof of concept, to prove the viability of such systems in aquatic environments. 

\subsection{Aims and Objectives}
The primary aim of this project is to develop a Proof of Concept system that enables the extraction of swimming metrics from video footage. The specific objectives are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item To design, prototype, and create a modular, under-water marker design that can be affixed to standard lane ropes.
    \item To create and annotate a small dataset of swimming footage with the newly-designed markers visible.
    \item To establish a pipeline for dynamic pixel-to-meter scale extraction using the lane rope markers for calibration.
    \item To calculate simple metrics such as swimmer velocity and stroke length using the calibrated scale. % or shall this objective be reserved for evaluation?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background Research}

Existing literature in sports biomechanics highlights the importance of kinematic metrics like stroke length and propulsion symmetry. Computer vision research has successfully applied deep learning for human pose estimation (HPE) and action recognition in water sports. However, a critical limitation in many CV-based swimming systems is the inability to convert pixel movement into absolute, real-world distances, often relying on relative metrics or requiring external sensors.

This project draws upon two key areas of research:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scale Estimation}: Research on using planar objects or known reference markers (fiducial markers) for depth and scale estimation is relevant, which we adapt by using the structured, repetitive nature of the lanerope.
    \item \textbf{Kinematic Segmentation and Symmetry}: Work on segmenting continuous athletic motion (like running or swimming) into discrete phases is essential for calculating time-based metrics (e.g., wall contact time) and assessing symmetry by comparing left and right limb kinematic profiles.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proposed Solution: System Overview}
The proposed system is partitioned into three functional steps: Lanerope Calibration, Feature Extraction, and System Output.

\subsection{Step 1: Lanerope Calibration (Validation Reference)}
This step is the foundation of the system, establishing a stable axis and scale for all downstream measurements. It involves a comparative study of two approaches:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Traditional CV Pipeline}: Utilising colour thresholding (to isolate the coloured floats), edge detection (e.g., Canny), and Hough Transform or RANSAC for line fitting to project a stable reference line along the lanerope's center.
    \item \textbf{CNN-based Pipeline}: Implementing a keypoint detection or semantic segmentation model (e.g., a customised U-Net or Mask R-CNN) to identify the boundary or center points of the lanerope floats. This is expected to offer better robustness against water splash and varying lighting.
\end{enumerate}
The results of these two pipelines will be used to create the Calibration Module, converting pixel-based distances into real-world meters.

\subsection{Step 2: Feature Extraction – Starts, Swim, and Turns}
With the metric scale in place, a robust pose-estimation model will track the swimmer's key joints. A sequence-modelling approach will then segment the footage to extract biomechanical features across the three domains:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Starts}: Metrics include reaction time, leap and flight times, underwater speed and distance, and shoulder/arm position at the end of the first pull.
    \item \textbf{Swim (Mid-Pool)}: Focus on stroke length (hand entry to exit in meters) and impulse (distance covered per stroke). This step will also facilitate the left–right symmetry assessment by comparing the angular and linear velocities of the corresponding limbs during the catch and pull phases.
    \item \textbf{Turns}: Metrics include stroke rate before the wall, entry-to-wall contact time, wall contact duration, and push-to-breakout measures (underwater distance and shoulder/arm positions leading to the second stroke).
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation Plan}
The evaluation strategy is two-fold, encompassing a rigorous quantitative assessment of the core technology and a qualitative validation of the final derived metrics.

\subsection{Quantitative Evaluation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lanerope Accuracy Comparison}: The two lanerope pipelines (Traditional CV and CNN) will be assessed against annotated ground truth. Metrics will include axis error (deviation from the true lanerope angle), scale error (difference from the known float interval distance), and frame-to-frame jitter (stability).
    \item \textbf{Downstream Metric Accuracy}: The lanerope method yielding the lowest error will be used to calibrate the system. The final system's metric outputs (e.g., a calculated 5-meter distance) will be compared to a physically measured distance to quantify the final distance estimation accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Qualitative Validation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Annotated Footage Comparison}: System outputs (keypoints, trajectories, and phase segmentations) will be overlaid onto the original video and validated against human-annotated samples.
    \item \textbf{Expert Feedback}: Structured interviews and feedback sessions will be conducted with certified swimming coaches to assess the interpretability, practical value, and actionability of the generated performance reports (e.g., symmetry score, wall contact duration).
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusions including Expected Outcomes and Difficulties/Challenges}
This project is expected to deliver a robust proof-of-concept system capable of providing high-value, objective biomechanical data from standard video. The expected outcomes include the validated optimal lanerope calibration method (Traditional CV or CNN) and a working pipeline that accurately calculates and visualises metrics across Starts, Swim, and Turns.

Key challenges and difficulties anticipated include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data Annotation}: Obtaining sufficient, high-quality, frame-by-frame annotated footage for training the CNN-based lanerope model and for ground-truthing the pose/phase segmentation.
    \item \textbf{Generalisation}: Ensuring the lanerope calibration method is robust against varying pool environments, lighting conditions, and camera angles (beyond a fixed setup).
    \item \textbf{Symmetry Complexity}: Developing a sufficiently sensitive metric to accurately quantify left–right symmetry, given the inherent small variances in human movement.
\end{itemize}


\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{AI in Sports Computer Vision}

Computer vision (CV) has fundamentally altered the landscape of sports analytics, transitioning from manual notation to automated, high-fidelity measurement. In the domain of modern sports science, AI-driven systems are now the standard for extracting kinematic data, estimating pose, and tracking athlete trajectories in real-time.

As noted in~\cite{zhou2025}, AI has emerged as a transformative tool in sports science, enabling objective, high-frequency measurements that surpass the limitations of traditional coaching. In particular, deep learning models have been used to automate event detection, action recognition, and performance prediction across a range of sports such as football, basketball, and athletics. While these advances demonstrate the potential of data-driven methods to tackle complex spatio-temporal patterns, the transfer of such techniques to an underwater setting remains a relatively under-explored domain.

Field sports provide stable visual environments, but aquatic environments\newline present unique noise factors. Consequently, sports analytics has emerged as a critical benchmark for evaluating the robustness and generalization capabilities of AI models. If a system can perform accurately in the chaotic visual domain of a swimming pool, it demonstrates a level of geometric reasoning that far exceeds standard requirements.

\subsection{Swimming as a Stress Test for AI Robustness}

While swimming analytics provides value to coaches, from a computer vision perspective, the pool environment serves as an adversarial domain that stress-tests the limits of image processing algorithms. The aquatic setting introduces non-linear distortions that are rarely encountered in terrestrial datasets.

The primary challenge is refraction. Light bending at the air-water interface creates unpredictable geometric distortions, effectively shifting the apparent location of underwater objects relative to the camera. This violates the pinhole camera model assumptions central to many standard calibration techniques. Furthermore, the environment is plagued by dynamic noise. As~\cite{giulietti2023} highlights in their abstract, measurements are ``strongly influenced by disturbing elements'', specifically bubbles, splashes, and surface reflections. These elements introduce severe occlusion and high-frequency visual noise that can confuse feature extractors trained on clean data.

Additionally, swimming pools lack standardization. Lane rope designs can vary in colour and sectioned lengths~\cite{FINA2025}, lighting conditions shift rapidly, and turbulence alters structural appearance. This constitutes a significant domain shift problem. For an AI model, a swimming pool is a chaotic, noisy environment, where the only way to achieve successful detection of structural features requires robust techniques.

\subsection{Traditional Computer Vision Approaches}

Historically, structural feature detection in swimming relied on handcrafted features and traditional image processing pipelines. These methods operate on the premise that semantic objects such as lane lines can be defined by explicit mathematical properties, for example, sharp intensity gradients or specific colour thresholds.

Common techniques include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Edge Detection \& Hough Transform: Algorithms like Canny or Sobel, combined with voting-based techniques to detect lines.
    \item Colour Segmentation: Early methods relied on handcrafted colour thresholds to isolate the rope's blue-and-white pattern or the red sections mandated by World Aquatics regulations~\cite{FINA2025}.
\end{itemize}

However, these methods break down very quickly. The alternating colour bands and non-uniform spacing of blue/white sections vary across pool designs, compromising robustness. As discussed in~\cite{holzmann2012}, traditional stereo vision often requires controlled conditions. In a pool, a Canny edge detector cannot distinguish between a lane rope edge and a turbulent wave, and colour segmentation fails when reflections alter the apparent hue.

\subsection{CNN-Based Detection and Segmentation}

The limitations of handcrafted features spurred the shift toward Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). Unlike traditional CV, CNNs learn hierarchical feature representations, allowing them to encode invariance to local noise like splashes or refraction.

Modern approaches employ CNNs for semantic segmentation of rope pixels, followed by post-processing to merge fragmented segments. Notably,~\cite{tran2024} demonstrates that a lightweight U-Net can recover the rope's position even when the water surface induces significant distortion. Similarly,~\cite{almajnoni2025} demonstrated the utility of YOLOv8 for swimmer stroke identification, proving that CNNs can track complex motion even in aquatic footage.

However, while CNNs offer superior robustness, the literature lacks a systematic investigation of how rope design variability impacts detection performance, especially when cameras are positioned at different angles or when the pool is subject to transient occlusions such as parts of the swimmers' body blocking the lane rope. 

\subsection{Pixel-to-Metre Scaling in Computer Vision}

Extracting metric data, such as metres per second, from 2D pixel coordinates is a fundamental geometric inference problem. Without a reference scale it is impossible to obtain meaningful real-world data; A velocity of x pixels per second means nothing when the real-world distance described by x pixels changes every frame in a video. 

\subsection*{Calibration Challenges}

~\cite{tran2024} describes a two-stage calibration strategy: first, estimating intrinsic parameters; second, computing a planar homography between the image plane and the pool floor using the rope as a known feature. The resulting homography yields a pixel-to-metre ratio applied uniformly across the frame. While effective for static setups, this strategy is influenced by the rope's colour fidelity and the refractive index of water, which can shrink or stretch the apparent rope width. %TODO: Revisit this section

Furthermore, World Aquatics' 2025 specifications~\cite{FINA2025} detail the width and spacing of the red sections of the lane rope which could be critical to acquiring a consistent calibration scale in certain scenarios, such as when the whole length of the lane rope is present in a video's frame. Unfortunately, in situations where this is not the case, different features must be considered.

\subsection{AI Methods for Geometric Reasoning}

Recent advancements in AI have moved beyond simple detection to geometric reasoning, that is, inferring 3D structure or metric scale from 2D images.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Monocular Depth Estimation: Models like MiDaS~\cite{chauhan2025} use deep learning to predict relative depth maps from single images, deriving depth cues that traditional geometry misses.
    \item Deep Distance Learning:~\cite{haseeb2018} introduced ``DisNet,'' estimating distance directly from bounding box parameters.
    \item Category-Level Pose Estimation:~\cite{lee2021} proposed frameworks that jointly estimate shape and metric scale for objects.
\end{itemize}

These ``geometry-aware'' networks suggest a pathway for swimming: instead of relying on brittle manual calibration or planar assumptions~\cite{zuo2021}, a neural network could infer the scale factor by analysing the semantic structure of the lane ropes, treating the scaling factor as a learnable parameter.

\subsection{Computer Vision for Swimming Analytics}

The end goal of many CV-based swimming studies is to recover motion-specific metrics.~\cite{tran2024} estimates distance travelled by tracking a swimmer's centre of mass, subsequently inferring velocity and average stroke length.~\cite{almajnoni2025} extends this by computing power output and kinetic energy through a hybrid CV-physics model that integrates 2-D pose estimates. Likewise,~\cite{cao2024} proposes a pose-estimation network trained on a large collection of swimming videos to localize key joints in the presence of water reflections.

These works illustrate that once spatial calibration is reliable, the task reduces to accurate temporal tracking. However, both studies note that surface turbulence and glare can degrade tracking accuracy. In particular, the rope's red bands—used as a visual cue for lap counting—introduce strong colour discontinuities that can mislead generic segmentation algorithms.

\subsection{Gap Analysis and Research Need}

Reviewing the convergence of sports CV and swimming analytics reveals distinct gaps, particularly regarding datasets and robust scaling.

\subsection*{Datasets and Benchmarks}
The paucity of publicly available datasets tailored to markerless swimming analyses has hindered reproducibility. SwimmerNET~\cite{giulietti2023} provides annotated 2-D keypoints but lacks underwater scenes. Moar~\cite{almajnoni2025} provides 3-D joint trajectories but relies on markers. Few datasets contain exhaustive labels for the rope's colour bands or the red sections mandated by~\cite{FINA2025}. Consequently, many studies train on limited subsets, limiting generalization.

\subsection*{Open Challenges}
The reviewed literature confirms that persistent challenges remain:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Optical Distortions: Refraction and glare render hand-crafted segmentation fragile~\cite{cao2024}.
    \item Design Variability: The red bands and non-uniform spacing of blue/white bands~\cite{competitorswim2022} introduce inter-pool variation which is acknowledged, but rarely accounted for in existing models.
    \item Absence of Automated Dynamic Scaling: Current analytics rely on manual calibration. There is no robust AI solution that utilizes the lane ropes themselves as dynamic calibration objects.
\end{enumerate}

These gaps highlight the need for a robust, generalizable AI pipeline that can detect lane rope features accurately and extract a stable pixel-to-metre scale. The present thesis builds upon the foundational work of~\cite{tran2024} and~\cite{cao2024}, extending it by developing a multiscale, attention-based segmentation module and integrating a physics-informed homography estimator.
