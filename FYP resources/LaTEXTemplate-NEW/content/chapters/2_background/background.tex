\chapter{Background}%
\label{chp:background}
The application of CV in sports analytics is a well-established and growing field, with extensive research focused on player tracking, pose estimation, and event recognition across various sports \cite{Naik2022, Chen2021}. However, the specific challenge of obtaining absolute distance metrics from a single, uncalibrated camera remains a key research area. Many existing systems rely on expensive sensor-based methods, such as Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs), or require complex, multi-camera setups with sophisticated camera calibration techniques \cite{Dubois2012}.

Recent research has explored vision-based swimming analysis, focusing on topics like swimmer segmentation, stroke type classification, and pose estimation \cite{Liao2020, Fani2018}. While these studies successfully analyze a swimmer's form and motion, they often fall short of providing absolute metrics like stroke length, as they lack a reliable method for converting pixel-based movement into real-world distance. Our proposed "Lanerope Method" fills this gap by utilizing a readily available fiducial marker (the lanerope) to establish a consistent and accurate spatial scale, allowing us to derive absolute kinematic data from simple video footage.

\section{Proposed Solution}
Our solution, the Lanerope Method, follows a structured computer vision pipeline.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Object Detection and Tracking}: A pre-trained object detection model (e.g., a YOLO-based architecture) will be fine-tuned to specifically identify the discrete float sections of the swimming lanerope in each video frame. Concurrently, a robust multi-object tracking algorithm will maintain the identity of each detected float across subsequent frames. This ensures a consistent spatial reference.

\item \textbf{Pixel-to-Distance Calibration}: Given that the physical dimensions of standard lanerope floats and their spacing are either known or can be measured, we will use the detected pixel locations of these floats to establish a consistent pixel-to-meter ratio. This ratio will be corrected for perspective distortion by analyzing the varying size and spacing of the floats as they recede into the distance. This step is critical for converting pixel movement to real-world distance.

\item \textbf{Swimmer Detection and Kinematic Calculation}: The system will then track the swimmer's position within the same video frames. With the calibrated spatial scale, the swimmer's pixel movement will be translated into meters. By measuring the distance traveled over time and correlating it with the swimmer's stroke cycle (e.g., using a simple stroke counter based on arm motion), we can derive key metrics like stroke length, swimming speed, and impulse.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Evaluation Plan}
The performance of the system will be evaluated using both qualitative and quantitative methods.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Quantitative Evaluation}: The primary evaluation will involve a controlled experiment where a known distance is physically measured within a pool lane. Video footage of a swimmer traversing this distance will be processed by our system. The system's calculated distance will then be compared against the known physical distance to determine the system's accuracy and margin of error. This will be repeated for multiple swimmers and varying camera angles to assess robustness.

\item \textbf{Qualitative Evaluation}: The derived kinematic metrics (e.g., stroke length) will be presented to a certified swimming coach for qualitative feedback. The coach's assessment of the swimmer's efficiency will be compared to the objective data provided by the system to ensure the metrics are meaningful and actionable.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusions including Expected Outcomes and Difficulties/Challenges}
This project is expected to result in a functional prototype that demonstrates the viability of the Lanerope Method for swimming performance analysis. The expected outcomes include a low-cost system that can accurately provide coaches and swimmers with objective kinematic data, thereby democratizing access to sports analytics.

The key challenges and difficulties anticipated include:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Occlusion}: The swimmer's body or the splash of water may temporarily obscure sections of the lanerope, making consistent tracking difficult.
\item \textbf{Lighting and Water Distortion}: Variable lighting conditions and the refraction of light in water could affect the accuracy of the object detection algorithms.
\item \textbf{Perspective Distortion}: As the camera is fixed, the perspective will change the appearance of the lanerope's floats as they get closer or further away. The calibration method must be robust enough to correct for this distortion.
\end{itemize}
Despite these challenges, we are confident that the proposed methodology offers a robust and innovative path to a widely accessible sports analysis tool.
